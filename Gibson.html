About Gibson

Gibson Brands, Inc., commonly known as Gibson, is one of the most renowned and enduring manufacturers of guitars and musical instruments in the world. Founded in 1902 by Orville Gibson in Kalamazoo, Michigan, the company initially focused on producing mandolins with innovative carved tops and backs. Gibson's shift to guitars in the 1930s, particularly archtop models like the L-5 and Super 400, laid the groundwork for its reputation as a premier instrument maker. By the mid-20th century, Gibson became a leader in the electric guitar market, thanks to its groundbreaking designs and collaborations with notable musicians.

The introduction of the Gibson Les Paul in 1952 marked a turning point in the company's history. Created in partnership with guitarist and inventor Les Paul, this solid-body electric guitar became a defining instrument in rock, blues, and jazz. Known for its rich, warm tones and sustain, the Les Paul has been favored by legendary musicians such as Jimmy Page, Slash, and Eric Clapton. Gibson also developed other iconic models like the SG, Flying V, Explorer, and ES-335, each contributing to the company's legacy and offering unique tonal characteristics and aesthetics.

Today, Gibson remains a major player in the music industry, headquartered in Nashville, Tennessee. Despite facing financial challenges and restructuring in recent years, the company has continued to innovate while honoring its heritage. It produces a wide range of instruments, from high-end Custom Shop models to more affordable Epiphone-branded guitars. With its storied history and a commitment to craftsmanship, Gibson continues to influence music across genres, maintaining its status as a symbol of excellence and creativity in the world of guitars.

Epiphone Brand

Epiphone, a subsidiary of Gibson Brands, Inc., is a historic guitar manufacturer known for producing high-quality, affordable instruments that cater to a wide range of musicians. Originally founded as the House of Stathopoulo in 1873 by Anastasios Stathopoulo, the company initially crafted lutes and violins before transitioning to mandolins and guitars. In 1928, the company was renamed Epiphone, blending "Epi," a nickname for Anastasios’s son Epaminondas, with "phone," meaning "voice" in Greek. During the 1930s and 1940s, Epiphone became a leading maker of archtop guitars, rivaling Gibson with its popular models such as the Emperor, Broadway, and Triumph.

In 1957, Gibson acquired Epiphone, which allowed the brand to focus on more affordable versions of Gibson’s iconic designs, such as the Les Paul, SG, and ES-335. Epiphone also continued producing its own distinctive models, including the Casino, which gained fame as a favorite of The Beatles. Epiphone's partnership with Gibson enabled it to deliver high-quality instruments at accessible price points, combining Gibson's innovation with Epiphone’s legacy of craftsmanship.

Today, Epiphone remains a vital part of the music world, offering instruments for players of all skill levels. Its product lineup includes everything from beginner-friendly models to premium guitars in collaboration with famous artists. Epiphone also produces reissues of vintage designs and maintains its commitment to quality and affordability. Whether for aspiring musicians or seasoned professionals seeking a reliable alternative to high-end Gibson models, Epiphone continues to uphold its reputation as a trusted name in the guitar industry.

Noteable Gibson Products
  • Les Paul

    The Gibson Les Paul is one of the most iconic and influential electric guitars in music history, known for its rich tone, sustain, and timeless design. Introduced in 1952, the Les Paul was the result of a collaboration between Gibson and guitarist-inventor Les Paul, who had been experimenting with solid-body electric guitar designs since the 1940s. Gibson sought to compete with Fender's groundbreaking Telecaster, and the Les Paul model combined innovative features with classic aesthetics, such as a carved maple top, solid mahogany body, and a single-cutaway shape.

    The original 1952 Les Paul featured P-90 single-coil pickups, a trapeze tailpiece, and a gold finish, earning it the nickname "Goldtop." Over the following years, the design evolved to include humbucking pickups (introduced in 1957), which significantly reduced noise while delivering a warmer, fuller sound. In 1958, the Les Paul Standard debuted with a sunburst finish and became highly sought after for its striking appearance and superior craftsmanship. While production of the Les Paul briefly ceased in the early 1960s in favor of the Gibson SG, its popularity resurged later in the decade, driven by iconic players like Eric Clapton, Jimmy Page, and Duane Allman.

    Today, the Les Paul remains a cornerstone of Gibson's lineup, available in a variety of configurations and price ranges to suit diverse players. From the high-end Custom Shop recreations of vintage models to the more accessible Studio and Tribute series, the Les Paul continues to inspire musicians across genres. Its combination of elegance, versatility, and historical significance ensures its enduring status as one of the most celebrated guitars in the world.

  • SG (Solid Guitar)

    The Gibson SG (Solid Guitar) is one of the most recognizable and enduring electric guitar models, celebrated for its lightweight design, sharp double-cutaway body, and aggressive tone. Introduced in 1961 as a redesign of the Les Paul, the SG was created to address issues like weight and playability in earlier models. Its sleek, contoured body and thinner profile made it more comfortable for players, while its double-horned design allowed for better access to higher frets. Though initially marketed as the “Les Paul SG,” it became known simply as the SG after Les Paul distanced himself from the model.

    The SG quickly gained popularity due to its tonal versatility and striking aesthetic. Its dual humbucking pickups produce a powerful, biting sound that excels in rock, blues, and metal. The SG’s distinctive tone and sustain are enhanced by its all-mahogany construction and set neck. Over the years, models like the SG Standard and SG Special became staples for guitarists seeking a combination of raw power and refined performance. Players such as Tony Iommi of Black Sabbath, Angus Young of AC/DC, and Derek Trucks of The Allman Brothers Band helped cement the SG's legacy as a rock and metal powerhouse.

    Today, the Gibson SG is available in various configurations, from vintage-inspired reissues to modern interpretations with updated features. Its enduring popularity lies in its unique blend of aesthetics, playability, and sonic range, making it a favorite for musicians across genres. As one of Gibson's flagship models, the SG continues to symbolize innovation and rock 'n' roll spirit, ensuring its place as a timeless classic.

  • ES-335

    The Gibson ES-335 is one of the most iconic electric guitars, renowned for its semi-hollow body design that bridges the gap between solid-body and hollow-body guitars. Introduced in 1958, the ES-335 ("Electric Spanish") was Gibson's first commercially successful semi-hollow guitar. Its innovative design features a solid maple center block running through the body, which reduces feedback at high volumes while retaining the warm, resonant tone of a hollow-body instrument. This combination of features made the ES-335 a versatile choice for jazz, blues, rock, and beyond.

    The ES-335's double-cutaway design, which allows easy access to higher frets, paired with dual humbucking pickups, provides a rich, balanced sound that can range from smooth and mellow to sharp and biting. Over the years, the ES-335 has become synonymous with some of the greatest guitarists in history, including Chuck Berry, B.B. King, Eric Clapton, and Larry Carlton. It has been a staple in genres ranging from classic rock and jazz fusion to soul and R&B, thanks to its adaptability and unique tonal qualities.

    Today, the ES-335 remains a flagship model in Gibson’s lineup, with various iterations tailored to different playing styles and budgets. From meticulously crafted Custom Shop reissues that replicate vintage models to more affordable versions like the Epiphone Dot, the ES-335 continues to inspire musicians worldwide. Its timeless design and versatile sound ensure its place as one of the most celebrated and enduring guitars in music history.

  • Flying V

    The Gibson Flying V is one of the most distinctive and innovative electric guitars, celebrated for its futuristic design and powerful tone. Introduced in 1958 as part of Gibson's "Modernistic Series," the Flying V was designed to push the boundaries of traditional guitar aesthetics and appeal to forward-thinking musicians. Its radical V-shaped body and sharp angles set it apart from conventional guitar designs of the time. Despite its striking appearance, the Flying V initially struggled to gain popularity and was discontinued after just a few years of limited production.

    The Flying V experienced a resurgence in the late 1960s and 1970s, as its bold look and aggressive sound caught the attention of rock and blues musicians. Guitarists like Albert King, Jimi Hendrix, and later, heavy metal pioneers like K.K. Downing and Michael Schenker, helped cement the Flying V's reputation as a rock icon. Its solid mahogany body and dual humbucking pickups produce a thick, resonant tone with excellent sustain, making it ideal for high-energy performances and heavy music.

    Today, the Flying V remains a staple in Gibson's lineup, revered for its blend of visual flair and sonic power. It is available in a range of configurations, from vintage-inspired models to modern versions with updated features like active pickups and contemporary hardware. The Flying V’s enduring appeal lies in its ability to stand out both visually and musically, ensuring its legacy as one of the most iconic guitars in history.
